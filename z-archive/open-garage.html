OpenGarage

Goal

Design an experience that would help either sellers or buyers get more from their garage sales.

Identify

I began by identifying the key players: sellers and buyers, listing out everything I knew about them and the initial problems they faced.

Sellers

  • Families
  • Creating more storage space
  • Getting rid of old or unused items
  • Wanting to earn some extra cash
  • Wanting to get a good price on their items
  • Events could be planned or unplanned

Sellers' Problems

Preparing for a sale is a daunting task

Buyers

  • Families or single
  • Looking for a deal
  • Might not know exactly what they are looking for
  • Variety of items
  • Fair price
  • Concerned with condition of items

Buyers' Problems

Difficult to find sales and purchase merchandise

Map

With this information about the two types of users I created a map to visualize their individual journeys.

Seller → Has something to sell → Hosts a garage sale → Makes all the money!

Buyer → Actively or passively looking → Sees a thing they want → Buys thing!

I decided to focus on the seller journey specifically around hosting a garage sale. Given more time to dive in, I would of looked into how this related to the buyer journey seeing where there were similarities and making that hand-off as seamless as possible.

Hypothesis

An app that allows sellers to easily upload images and advertise their physical garage sales will make it easier to host a garage sale, increase customer traffic, and drive more sales.

Research

I looked at examples of similar platforms for buyers and sellers, analyzing patterns and systems that I thought were effective. Snkrs and Canopy used large images which focuses a buyers attention, potentially leading to more sales. Yelp uses a nearby filter lets a user know how close they are to a restaurant increasing the chances of a user visiting that restaurant. I had a clear focus on the seller journey which helped narrow the features relevant to sellers.

Based on that research, I sketched/listed out all the digital features that would make a seller successful at their physical garage sale:

  • Easy to upload photos
  • Easy to show location of garage sale
  • Times and dates of garage sale
  • Easy to share garage sale
  • Pricing of items

Storyboard

From there I began to storyboard out how a seller might discover, download, and create a garage sale using OpenGarage.

  1. User sees ad on social media for a garage sale app
  2. Download OpenGarage from app store
  3. On launch, OpenGarage shows examples of garage sales happening nearby. Seeing examples will help a seller visualize how they would set up their own garage sale.
  4. Seller sees the CTA to "host a garage sale"
  5. OpenGarage asks for name, email, location, date of event (OpenGarage will use this information to populate event)
  6. The garage sale screen will asks for name of event and prompt user to add photos
  7. OpenGarage asks to access photos, user can take photos or choose from photo library to begin building inventory for garage sale
  8. User selects photos
  9. Item cards will build in prompting the user to name item, identify price, and comment on condition.
  10. Once the photos have been added, users can publish their garage sale
  11. There will be a prompt with a link to share on social media
  12. After their event has been uploaded they are taken back to the initial screen with a list of nearby garage sales

Prototype

After putting together the storyboard I needed to validate the solutions I believed the app was accomplishing. I had one tester run through the prototype which is not nearly as many data points as I would like but it did reveal some findings. I gave the tester a prompt of downloading an app that would help them create a garage sale. I then asked them to create a garage sale once they downloaded the app.

High Fidelity Mock

I chose to focus on the add item feature, specifically the adding information and saving actions. As a user selects images to add to their OpenGarage event they are prompted to add additional information about each item. If multiple images are selected cards build up and stack behind the first image. There are three actions, saving, deleting, and skipping. When a item is saved, a blue border appears around the card and the entire card is whisked away to the right of the screen. If the item is deleted is moves to the left of the screen. When all cards have been saved, deleted, or skipped, a final card appears denoting that the user is finished with this task and prompts the user to view and publish their event.

Learnings

Onboarding
While conducting the test, it became clear that launching the tester into a "facebook-type" screen was confusing. I think clearer instructions would have helped here. I also think an initial onboarding would help orient a user and guide them to view garage sales or create their own.

Call to action
The goal of this experience was to enable a seller to easily host a garage sale. There are two ways to achieve this goal: a "Host your own garage sale" CTA near the top of the screen and an "Add event" icon in the nav bar below. The CTA was visually smaller than most of the elements on the page and was easily skipped over.

Updates
The rest of the flow went as expected, the tester was able to add photos and begin a garage sale. Some clear next steps are to add a simple onboarding screen to help orient a new user. Ensure the host event CTA has more prominent and include the created garage sale on the final screen.

Final Thoughts

This solves a few of the problems experienced by sellers. If an app can allow easy uploading of photos and can create and event with a few taps the experience of hosting a garage sale will less daunting. This would also help with marketing as sellers can share their events on social media easily. Outside of an app, OpenGarage would send sellers a Host Kit which would include materials for signs, markers, flags, shirts, square reader, and other elements that would help with a seamless event experience.
